A4: Press fit box

Tolerance Testing

I thought the hardest part of making a press-fit box would be tolerance, so I started by printing a push-fit tolerance board from here. I printed with the default standard quality settings on Cura. The pieces fit at .075mm, but I figured there would be some level of tolerance stacking and because they were pressing in a cube arrangement, 0.1mm would be an acceptable place to start. To avoid printing a bunch of things that didn’t fit together, I printed two “2-slot” pieces that could be fit together flat or at a corner.

Push-fit tolerance board

Initial 2-slot pieces demonstrating a flat fit

Initial 2-slot pieces demonstrating a corner fit

Test V1 – .1mm, standard settings in Cura

This test was a tight fit, but it fit. They could be clicked together with a satisfying snap, but it was clear that there was some horizontal expansion. On a flat fit, the “bottom” sides needed a lot of wiggling to slot together while the “top” sides worked perfectly. On a corner fit with the “bottom” facing in, I could barely get the pieces together, but its the “bottom” facing out, it was an easy fit. On a corner fit, I did notice some gaps on the interior joint where the inside corners of the slots naturally rounded so couldn’t accommodate the the entire “prong”. A dog-bone type cut could solve this problem, but I had different ideas.

Test V2 –.1mm, standard settings in Cura with shorter “slot”

For this test, I ran the same settings but removed .1mm from the end of the “slot” to see if I could get the prong to hit the end of the join. I don’t know why I thought this would work – I just had to do it to realize it wouldn’t. It didn’t, and the gap was worse.

Even worse joint gaps

Sidebar – Expansion cubes

I pivoted to instead address the horizontal expansion issue, hoping that would solve for a better fit. I printed two cubes – one with default standard settings, and one with -.15mm horizontal expansion compensation on the first 4 layers, plus I reduced the print speed to 50% for the first 4 layers (shell). You can see the clear difference between the two cubes in the image below – horizontal expansion compensation worked to cure the dreaded elephant foot effect!

Flawed cube on the left, less flawed cube on the right

Test V3 – .1mm vs .15mm tolerance, with horizontal expansion

Maybe the slot needs to be bigger? Based on my press fit tolerance test kit, I tested .15mm tolerance with the horizontal expansion set to -.15mm for the bottom layers. The bigger tolerance value was the clear winner, and fit much better but still had some tiny issues with the “bottom” sides fitting together. I printed the bottom and sides of a cube and the “gap” issue was almost completely fixed so I knew I was on to something.

Almost successful, but the bottoms were still tight.

Test V4 – .15mm tolerance, -.15mm horizontal expansion adjustment, .4mm initial layer height

For this test, I returned to my V3 settings but changed the initial layer height to .4mm match the nozzle width, based on some things I read on the internet here and here. The pieces had a satisfying snap-fit, very minimal gaps, and could be fit in any configuration of top/bottm match. Perfect!

Super duper fit!

Box Printing

It did take me a while to design the actual boxes in Rhino (see image below), but once they were designed, I had an uneventful box printing save for a bit of warping on the bottom panel which I ended up re-printing. The box was printed with .15mm tolerance, -.15mm horizontal expansion for the bottom shell, 4 slow initial layers, and .4mm initial layer height. I used hairspray for extra adhesion. The box press-fits nicely together and holds sugar without leaking.

Other thoughts

  • I may have been able to get away with a smaller tolerance but higher horizontal expansion compensation – something to consider in the future
  • Always print tiny bits and figure if they fit together before you print the big part! This is true for laser cutting, 3d printing… it’s probably true for everything and makes iteration so much less frustrating.
