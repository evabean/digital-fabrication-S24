A1: Adventures in Carboard Thickness

The sun/waves vessel from above

Phase 1: The sun and the waves

My original plan was to build a vessel-type structure with rotational symmetry of 30 “waves” held together by two “suns”. I drew a quick sketch to visualize what components I would need and to make sure I would meet the assignment requirements (30 repeated identical pieces with slot fits that pass a shape test). I opted to do my design in illustrator because that's where I can work quickly – I'll learn Rhino next week, I think!

Phase 2: Excited about thick cardboard

I was very excited when I acquired some sturdy corrugated cardboard for my A1 project. I managed to preserve a nice amount of ~7.5mm thick cardboard from a table saw box with the intention of using it to build my A1 project.

I took some measurements of the cardboard. I knew it wouldn't be perfect, because some of the cardboard was squashed in the journey, but I hoped it was close enough. I selected the least visibly squished pieces and used the calipers to take some measurements. The results were... mixed!

Squishy cardboard variance

Edge running parallel to corrugated ridges

I'm still practicing measuring, and I'm sure that had something to do with it, but I measured a range of thicknesses from 7.47mm to 7.7mm. I also noticed the thickest measurement was on the edge running parallel to the corrugated ridges, and I wondered if I would see a lot of difference between different edges.

Phase 3: Cut tests and not exited about thick cardboard

To test that theory, and play with how kerf works, I made a template that starts at the widest cardboard size I measured and moves down in .05mm increments (there was a lot of variance on "the internet forums" about kerf for cardboard, but not a lot of consensus, so I tried to cover a range knowing I might have to increase or decrease my variance scale). I planned to cut two versions of it, just to see how consistent the same cuts at two different times would be.

The cardboard is pretty thick, and I couldn't find any specs online for settings to cut through. I tried a few test cuts with varying speed/power/frequency until I found one that cut through without too much burn. The winning combination was 5% speed, 100% power, and 80% frequency.However, when I went to cut my kerf test template at this setting, my cardboard started flaming a little too much for my comfort. It was time to abandon the thick cardboard.

Cut tests on thick cardboard. The last setting worked but was quite burnt.

A failed and quite burnt kerf template test cut

Phase 4: Cut Tests And Excited About Thinner Cardboard

A classmate was kind enough to give me her leftover thin cardboard, so I measured it and then set to work doing some new test cuts. I started with the cardboard settings recommended on the materials sheet above the printers, and tweaked until I got a good cut with little burn. I found that sometimes 20s/50p/50f worked and other times it didn’t – my final working cut was 20s/50p/50f but with a second pass of the laser, on the advice of the nice man at the front desk.

Measuring thin cardboard with calipers

Kerf template cut tests on thinner cardboard

With my kerf test piece cut, I determined that a 1.8mm gap was perfect to pass a wiggle test, with about .7mm of cardboard vaporized during cutting.

Shake test!

Phase 5: Dialed in and assembly

With my cut width dialed in, I mass-cut 30-something waves and six suns. I made a few extra each in case I encountered issues assembling. I had started with the 20s/50p/50f double cuts, but I experimented again and found that 15s/60p/50f was a very reliable configuration for 2.5mm cardboard on the large laser printer. I was able to lift the “sun” right off the printer without any catching.

What I learned

About speed, power, and frequency on cardboard

In my experiments, it seemed like speed had the most impact on cardboard burn ability, in that order. When I dialed the speed down to 5-10% to cut through the thick cardboard, it was visibly smokier and flamier than cuts above 50% speed. The high power was able to cut through the thick cardboard, but when I lowered it to around 50% on my second set of tests, the cuts were much cleaner and less burnt.

About kerf

My original kerf test had .05mm increments, and on the few I was able to test, they were all way too big to get a tight fit. I scaled it up to .1mm increments and started .5mm lower than the measured thickness for the thinner cardboard and had much better luck. I feel like I have a good sense of tolerance for two-ply cardboard now, but am curious to see other peoples’ results for different thicknesses and plys.

About coruggation and weak spots

When I went to go assemble the piece I discovered that putting in multiple pieces beside each other would “squish” the tab and leave less room for the adjacent piece. It got pretty tight as I went on, and I ended up pinching the edges of the cardboard to slot them in. The strongest spots seemed to have diagonal corrugation support, and the weakest had corrugation running across horizontally. This is a fun corrugation specific problem, in my estimate!
